Introduction 
The purpose of this project is to conduct a detailed financial and strategic comparison 
between ExxonMobil Corporation (XOM) and Chevron Corporation (CVX), two of the 
largest integrated energy companies in the world. Both firms operate across the upstream 
(exploration and production) and downstream (refining and marketing) segments. 
ExxonMobil also maintains additional exposure to chemical and specialty product lines, 
while Chevron focuses on renewable initiatives and carbon capture technologies. This 
study evaluates each company’s financial performance for Fiscal Year 2024, using data 
from SEC Form 10-K filings, Yahoo Finance, and MSN Money. 
The analysis evaluates liquidity, solvency, profitability, operational efficiency, and 
shareholder value creation through a combination of ratio analysis, DuPont 
decomposition, and valuation modeling. Each section builds toward a comprehensive 
financial assessment, starting with liquidity and leverage comparisons, then exploring 
business structure and management discussion insights, and concluding with Discounted 
Cash Flow (DCF) and Dividend Discount Model (DDM) valuations. By integrating both 
quantitative and qualitative perspectives, this report aims to determine which company 
shows stronger financial health, operational efficiency, and long-term growth potential in 
today’s evolving energy landscape.

The current ratio, quick ratio, total debt ratio, and debt-to-equity ratio show that both 
Chevron and ExxonMobil maintain strong liquidity and conservative capital structures. 
However, Chevron demonstrates slightly better balance sheet strength across leverage 
metrics (Chevron Corporation, 2024; ExxonMobil Corporation, 2024). Both companies 
report nearly identical current ratios of 0.98 for Chevron and 0.97 for ExxonMobil, 
indicating adequate short-term liquidity with current assets roughly equal to current 
liabilities. The quick ratios are also similar: 0.67 for Chevron and 0.65 for ExxonMobil. 
The leverage ratios differentiate their financial strategies. Chevron’s total debt ratio of 
10.4% is slightly lower than ExxonMobil’s 11.8%, and its debt-to-equity ratio of 17% vs. 
Exxon’s 23% reflects a more conservative capital approach. ExxonMobil’s slightly 
higher leverage suggests that management is comfortable using modest debt levels to 
finance large-scale operations and capital-intensive projects, such as its chemicals 
manufacturing business. 
Business Lines, MD&A & DuPont Analysis 
Primary lines of business (from 10-K) 
ExxonMobil (XOM): The Form 10-K identifies four main segments: Upstream, Energy 
Products (Refining & Marketing), Chemical Products, and Specialty Products (such as 
lubricants). 
Chevron (CVX): Chevron reports two principal segments: Upstream (exploration & 
production) and Downstream (refining & marketing). Chemical activities are mainly 
conducted through its CPChem equity affiliate (Chevron SEC Report, 2024).

• Margins: Exxon’s FY2024 net margin (~10.2%) is higher than Chevron’s 
(~8.7%). This aligns with Exxon’s broader integration (Chemicals & Specialty) that 
supports consolidated profitability. 
• Asset efficiency: Exxon’s asset turnover (0.79×) beats Chevron’s (0.78×),

• Leverage: Both firms run conservative leverage (equity multipliers ~1.6–1.7×), 
consistent with the low (net) debt-to-capital targets discussed in MD&A. 
 
Exhibit B - DuPont (5-step, FY2024) 
We expand profit margin into: Tax burden (NI/EBT) × Interest burden (EBT/EBIT) × 
Operating margin (EBIT/Sales). 
 
From Yahoo/company income statements: both companies show low interest expense 
relative to EBIT, so interest burden ≈ 0.95–0.98 (minimal drag). Effective tax rates are 
broadly in the mid-20s, making tax burden ≈ 0.75–0.80. The differentiator is operating 
margin (EBIT/Sales), where Exxon’s integrated mix tends to run a slightly higher EBIT 
margin than Chevron in 2024, given downstream margin pressure at Chevron. 
 
Bottom line on ROE drivers (5-step view) 
● Exxon’s higher ROE is driven primarily by operating margin and asset turnover, 
not by leverage or taxes. 
● Chevron trails mainly on EBIT/Sales and marginally on turnover in 2024; the 
leverage policy is similarly conservative.

What does this means?  
 
• Business model contrast: Exxon’s four-segment integration provides 
earnings resilience and throughput advantages; Chevron’s simpler two-
segment profile leans more heavily on Upstream and downstream margin 
conditions. 
 
• Dominance: On revenue, net income, assets, and reserves/resources 
breadth, Exxon is the larger, more diversified operator in FY2024. 
 
• MD&A framing: Both management teams use (net) debt-to-capital as the 
headline leverage metric. Exxon’s net leverage is lower; Chevron pairs 
low leverage with very high shareholder returns (buybacks + dividends). 
 
• ROE drivers: 2024 ROE = ~14% (XOM) vs ~11% (CVX). The profit 
margin (EBIT/Sales) and asset turnover-leverage is not the story.

Investment Call

higher margin of safety under DCF assumptions. Chevron remains a strong dividend

Key risks include oil price volatility, capital intensity, and the ongoing energy transition.

Conclusion 
The comparative analysis identifies ExxonMobil as the more dominant and financially 
stable energy major in 2024. While both firms maintain strong balance sheets, Chevron’s 
lower leverage slightly improves its liquidity position. However, ExxonMobil’s higher 
profitability, stronger asset utilization, and superior ROE (13.95% vs. 11.21%) highlight 
its operational advantage. 
Valuation results reinforce this position: ExxonMobil’s DCF-based fair value ($130–140) 
indicates moderate undervaluation, while Chevron’s ($155–160) suggests limited upside. 
Both maintain disciplined capital structures and shareholder-focused strategies, but 
ExxonMobil’s diversified business model provides greater resilience in shifting market 
conditions. 
Final Recommendation: ExxonMobil is rated BUY, and Chevron is rated HOLD. 
ExxonMobil’s integrated operations, consistent free cash flow, and lower relative risk 
position it as the stronger long-term investment opportunity.
