Introduction 
The Boeing 737 MAX crisis represents one of the most significant ethical failures in 
modern corporate and engineering history. Two fatal crashes in 2018 and 2019 resulted in the 
deaths of 346 individuals and exposed systemic weaknesses in corporate governance, 
regulatory oversight, and organizational culture. Subsequent reviews by the Federal Aviation 
Administration (FAA) and the U.S. House Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure 
identified deficiencies in certification oversight, risk communication, and organizational 
decision-making processes (FAA, 2022; U.S. House Committee on Transportation and 
Infrastructure, 2020). Separately, the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) examined Boeing’s 
representations to regulators in connection with the aircraft’s certification.  
 
In July 2024, the DOJ announced that Boeing had reached an agreement in principle to 
resolve a criminal charge related to certification representations; however, the proposed plea 
agreement was later rejected by a federal judge in December 2024, reflecting continued judicial 
scrutiny (U.S. Department of Justice [DOJ], 2024). In November 2025, the court approved the 
DOJ’s motion to dismiss the criminal case under a revised agreement, a development that 
generated controversy among victims’ families and observers (Reuters, 2025). These 
developments underscore the gravity of ethical, legal, and governance breakdowns within the 
organization. The Boeing case provides a powerful framework for examining the interaction of 
professional ethics, corporate governance, technological complexity, and stakeholder 
responsibility in safety-critical industries.

Case Identification and Organizational Context 
Historically, Boeing was widely recognized as an engineering-driven organization that 
prioritized safety, technical excellence, and conservative design philosophy. However, over the 
past several decades, analysts and scholars have noted a gradual cultural shift following 
corporate restructuring and leadership transitions that increasingly emphasized cost control, 
shareholder value, and competitive positioning (Mintz & Miller, 2025). This shift did not occur 
abruptly. Rather, it emerged incrementally through incentive structures that rewarded 
production speed, financial performance, and market competitiveness more strongly than 
technical prudence and system redundancy. 
 
The redesign of the 737 platform into the 737 MAX illustrates how competitive 
pressure shaped decision-making. In response to Airbus’s A320neo, Boeing opted to update an 
existing airframe rather than develop an entirely new aircraft. Larger engines were repositioned 
further forward on the wings, altering aerodynamic characteristics and creating a tendency for 
the aircraft’s nose to pitch upward under certain conditions (U.S. House Committee on 
Transportation and Infrastructure, 2020). To address this issue, Boeing implemented the 
Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS), an automated flight control 
software intended to adjust the aircraft’s nose position. However, MCAS relied on input from a 
single angle-of-attack sensor, creating a critical single point of failure (Federal Aviation 
Administration [FAA], 2022). When erroneous sensor data triggered MCAS repeatedly, pilots 
encountered automated system behavior they had not been fully informed about or trained to 
manage.

The ethical dimension deepened because pilots were not fully informed of MCAS 
functionality, nor was simulator training universally mandated. Investigations later revealed 
that certain system characteristics were not sufficiently disclosed during certification processes 
(U.S. Department of Justice [DOJ], 2024). The result was not simply a software malfunction 
but a breakdown in transparency, risk management, and professional responsibility. 
 
Stakeholder Implications 
The Boeing case implicates multiple stakeholder groups. Passengers possessed a 
fundamental right to safety and informed consent regarding aircraft systems. Pilots and flight 
crews had the right to accurate operational manuals, comprehensive disclosure of aircraft 
systems, and appropriate training. Engineers within Boeing held professional obligations to 
prioritize public safety in accordance with established engineering ethics principles (Herkert et 
al., 2020). Senior executives and board members bore fiduciary duties not only to shareholders 
but to all stakeholders whose lives and livelihoods depended upon safe aircraft manufacturing. 
The FAA, as the primary regulator, carries the responsibility of independent oversight to ensure 
compliance with safety standards. These intersecting responsibilities illustrate how failures in 
governance affect multiple constituencies simultaneously (Mintz & Miller, 2025). 
 
The Organizational Designation Authorization (ODA) structure permitted Boeing 
employees to act on behalf of the FAA in certain certification processes. While this delegation 
was designed to improve administrative efficiency, congressional investigations raised 
concerns about weakened oversight and the concentration of certification authority within the 
manufacturer (U.S. House Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, 2020). The

Ethical Framework Analysis 
The central ethical dilemma confronting Boeing leadership can be framed as a conflict 
between competitive survival and uncompromising safety assurance. Under intense market 
pressure from Airbus, leadership faced incentives to accelerate certification and avoid costly 
training requirements that might disadvantage the aircraft commercially. The ethical question 
becomes whether short-term financial and competitive benefits can ever justify incremental 
safety risk in an industry where failures result in catastrophic loss of life (Federal Aviation 
Administration [FAA], 2022; U.S. House Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, 
2020). 
 
From a deontological perspective, the answer is unequivocal. Duty-based ethics asserts 
that human life must never be treated as a variable within a cost-benefit calculation. Boeing had 
an absolute moral obligation to ensure that identifiable safety risks were thoroughly mitigated 
prior to aircraft deployment (Herkert et al., 2020). This duty extends beyond regulatory 
minimum compliance to proactive risk mitigation. 
 
A narrow utilitarian analysis might initially suggest that preserving jobs, market share, 
and economic stability could justify expedited production. However, a comprehensive 
utilitarian framework requires the inclusion of all stakeholders and all consequences. Once 
catastrophic loss of life, global reputational damage, criminal penalties, and long-term

Page 6
6  
  
economic loss are incorporated into the calculation, the utilitarian case collapses (FAA, 2022). 
The aggregate harm vastly exceeded any short-term economic benefit. 
 
Rights theory further clarifies the ethical violation. Passengers and pilots had the right 
to full disclosure of system behavior affecting flight control (U.S. Department of Justice [DOJ], 
2024). Withholding material information about MCAS functionality undermined informed 
consent and violated these rights. From a virtue ethics standpoint, the case reflects deficiencies 
in organizational courage, prudence, and transparency. Ethical organizations cultivate cultures 
where dissent is encouraged, and safety concerns are elevated rather than suppressed. 
 
Technology, Automation Bias, and Information Asymmetry 
The role of technology in the crisis highlights the interaction between software systems 
and organizational culture. Automation bias refers to the tendency to over-rely on automated 
systems under the assumption that algorithmic intervention enhances safety (Herkert et al., 
2020). In the 737 MAX case, excessive confidence in MCAS performance contributed to the 
underestimation of failure risk (Federal Aviation Administration [FAA], 2022; U.S. House 
Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, 2020). Moreover, the reliance on a single 
angle-of-attack sensor contradicted long-standing aviation redundancy principles (FAA, 2022). 
Information asymmetry compounded the problem. Pilots were placed in situations 
where automated override behavior occurred without full knowledge of system design logic 
(U.S. Department of Justice [DOJ], 2024). Ethical system implementation requires that end 
users possess adequate information to respond to risk scenarios. Technology does not operate

Evaluation of Alternative Courses of Action 
One potential course of action available to Boeing leadership was to maintain 
production schedules while incrementally addressing safety concerns. This profit-centric 
approach offered short-term advantages, including preservation of cash flow, avoidance of 
contractual penalties, and maintenance of shareholder confidence. However, it sustained 
systemic risk exposure and increased the likelihood of further catastrophic failure. Subsequent 
regulatory reviews and investigations highlighted the extensive financial, operational, and 
reputational consequences that followed the crashes (Federal Aviation Administration [FAA], 
2022). 
 
An alternative course of action involved halting production to conduct a comprehensive 
engineering-driven safety reset. This approach would include redesigning MCAS with sensor 
redundancy, mandating simulator training, separating safety reporting lines from production 
management, and fully cooperating with regulators. Although this strategy entails short-term 
financial losses and market vulnerability, it addresses root causes rather than surface symptoms 
(FAA, 2022; U.S. Department of Justice [DOJ], 2024). 
 
A comparative analysis suggests that the engineering-driven safety reset is ethically 
superior. It satisfies deontological duty by prioritizing human life, fulfills stakeholder

obligations by restoring transparency, and produces stronger long-term utilitarian outcomes by 
reducing systemic risk and preserving institutional trust (Mintz & Miller, 2025). 
 
Selected Course of Action and Justification 
The most ethically defensible and strategically sustainable course of action is a 
comprehensive engineering-driven safety reset supported by enhanced transparency and 
strengthened regulatory collaboration (Federal Aviation Administration [FAA], 2022; U.S. 
Department of Justice [DOJ], 2024). This approach requires not merely technical correction but 
cultural transformation. Safety authority should be structurally independent of production 
targets, and engineers must be empowered to raise concerns without retaliation. Executive 
compensation structures should incorporate safety performance metrics alongside financial 
indicators to realign organizational incentives (Mintz & Miller, 2025). 
 
The short-term financial drawbacks of this strategy are significant. Production delays, 
contractual penalties, and temporary reputational exposure are likely consequences of 
comprehensive reform (FAA, 2022). However, financial losses are recoverable through 
operational improvement and restored market confidence. The loss of human life and erosion 
of public trust are not reversible. Accordingly, ethical analysis supports the conclusion that the 
benefits of comprehensive reform outweigh the costs. 
 
Governance and Cultural Reform 
The deeper failure in the Boeing case reflects weaknesses in governance architecture. 
Effective oversight in safety-critical industries requires board-level expertise capable of

Page 9
9  
  
challenging technical assumptions and scrutinizing safety-related decisions (Mintz & Miller, 
2025). Incentive structures that disproportionately reward stock performance and delivery 
volume can create moral hazard by distorting organizational priorities away from risk 
mitigation (Mintz & Miller, 2025). Reforms should therefore include stronger board 
engagement in safety oversight, independent safety reporting channels, and reevaluation of 
delegated regulatory authority structures that concentrate certification authority within the 
manufacturer (U.S. House Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, 2020). 
 
Cultural reconstruction is equally critical. Ethical erosion occurs gradually when 
organizational silence replaces professional dissent and when performance pressures 
discourage the escalation of safety concerns (Mintz & Miller, 2025). Establishing a formal 
“Safety Stop” authority, whereby any engineer can halt production without fear of retaliation, 
reinforces psychological safety and ethical courage. Transparency with regulators and the 
public should be institutionalized as a permanent operating principle rather than deployed only 
in response to crisis (Federal Aviation Administration [FAA], 2022). 
 
Long-Term Ethical Implications 
Organizational trust functions as a critical intangible asset in safety-critical industries. 
Airlines and passengers depend on sustained confidence in engineering integrity and regulatory 
compliance. Research in corporate governance suggests that organizations with strong ethical 
cultures experience greater long-term resilience, reduced litigation exposure, and stronger 
stakeholder loyalty (Mintz & Miller, 2025). The 737 MAX crisis illustrates that ethical 
compromise is not only morally indefensible but also strategically unsustainable.

In safety-critical industries, legitimacy derives from demonstrated commitment to 
protecting human life. When leadership allows financial metrics to eclipse moral responsibility, 
institutional fragility emerges and long-term viability is threatened (Mintz & Miller, 2025). The 
Boeing case therefore serves as a cautionary example for technology-driven organizations 
operating under competitive pressure. 
 
Conclusion 
The erosion of safety culture at Boeing reflects the convergence of competitive 
pressure, automation overconfidence, regulatory weakness, and misaligned incentives. The 
profit-centric strategy was ethically flawed and strategically shortsighted. A comprehensive 
engineering-driven safety reset grounded in transparency, stakeholder responsibility, and 
governance reform represents the only defensible path forward (Mintz & Miller, 2025). 
 
In aviation, safety is not a negotiable variable within financial calculus. It is the 
foundational principle upon which corporate legitimacy rests. Sustainable success requires 
deliberate reinforcement of engineering integrity, transparent leadership, independent oversight, 
and unwavering commitment to human life. The Boeing case ultimately demonstrates that 
ethical governance is not merely a compliance requirement but a strategic necessity in safety-
critical industries.
